1.2 Company policies

The top management of the Company has adopted a Policy of operating its “Ship Management Service” business under the provision of a comprehensive Quality, Health, Safety and Environmental Management System, implemented and operated to the requirements of ISO 9001:2015, ISO 14001:2015, ISO 45001:2018 and the ISM Code. It is company policy to continuously operate to these requirements, as would apply to it and where required (both as mandatory and value addition) have the systems certified by Accredited Certification bodies.

The Top Management has ensured that it’s Policies:

  • Are established implemented and maintained in consultation with workers at all levels of the shore-based and shipboard organisation.
  • Are appropriate to the ‘context’, purpose, nature and scale of its business and supports its
  • Strategic direction
  • Covers all activities undertaken by the Company
  • Includes commitments to comply with applicable requirements
  • Has provisions to continually improve the effectiveness of its Management Systems
  • Provides for a framework for setting and reviewing its objectives
  • Are communicated to all levels of the organisation and to all persons working for or on behalf of the company, including relevant interested parties

The Company reviews the policies at Management Review meetings for their continued suitability through processes described therein.

The Company implements its policies in practice through the application of formal procedures and instructions.

The CEO duly signs all important policies. All policies are available in this document.

Ships must post the following policies in hard copy near the safety notice board:

  • Quality policy
  • Shipboard drug and alcohol policy
  • Environmental policy
  • Security Policy
  • Health Safety and Risk Management Policy
  • Social Media Policy

The Company’s top management has Designated members of management who, irrespective of other responsibilities, have defined roles, responsibilities and authority for:

  • Direct access to senior management and the authority to implement suitable controls.

Alternate Designated Person has been appointed to ensure availability of a Designated Person.

Master’s responsibility and authority

The Ship’s Master has the complete overriding authority and responsibility for taking all necessary actions for safety, pollution and the efficient operation of his ship and to request the Company’s assistance as may be necessary. In certain circumstances this may mean deviating from documented procedures.

The Owner, Charterer, or the Company shall not prevent the Master from taking, or executing any decision which, in the Master’s professional judgment, is necessary for safe navigation and protection of the environment.

The Company has clearly defined and documented the Master’s responsibility with regard to:

  • Implementing the occupational health, Safety, Health and Environmental-protection and Quality policy(ies) of the Company;
  • Motivating the crew in the observation of the policy;
  • Issuing appropriate orders and instructions in a clear and simple manner;
  • Verifying that specified requirements are observed; and reviewing the management system and reporting its deficiencies to the shore- based management.

Masters are to take into account that Safety concerns will primarily prevail over security concerns should such a situation arise.

The Master ensures that the vessel meets the current international regulations for pollution prevention as well as appropriate local port state regulations. He is to ensure that proper on board training programs are conducted and vessel is fully prepared to deal with all emergency situations.

He is also overall responsible for the Security of the ship and co-ordinates with the CSO/SSO actively in this regard.

Security

It is the aim of the Company to provide a secure working environment for all employees and contractors.

The Company policy is to protect people, assets, and operations, from risk of injury and loss or damage from criminal, hostile or malicious acts.

All employees and contracts are expected to comply with the company security measures, and always within the law.

On-board Ships

Vessel Security is based on the International Ship and Port Facility Security Code (ISPS Code). To this end, every vessel coming into full management is provided with an approved ship specific ‘Ship Security Plan’ (SSP).

The procedure and provision for ‘visitors to vessels’ is clearly defined in the SSP, and is to be followed at all times in all ports.

Sea worthiness and navigational safety

The safety of personnel, the vessel, the cargo and the environment is paramount in navigation and vessel operations.

Prior to departure from any port, the vessel shall, in all respects, be in seaworthy condition with the cargo correctly stowed and properly secured.

Notwithstanding ‘Custom of the Trade’, the Master is responsible for ensuring that the vessel maintains a safe under keel clearance (UKC) and remains afloat at all times.

Every vessel is to be navigated in a safe and prudent manner at all times, and in strict compliance with the International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea, and any other applicable National or International rules and regulations.

Departure from practice demanded by this Policy and its supporting procedures may be made for the protection of life, the protection of property, the prevention of pollution, or to permit compliance with the law.

All navigational equipment including lights, compass, communications and signalling equipment is maintained fully operational. The company documents all defects and corrective actions. The company employs suitably trained staff capable of maintaining navigational equipment or has such staff available under contract.

Ref: Navigan safety, Vessel operations and Safety management Manual

Cargo and Ballast Operations (LNG)

Cargo operations shall be performed safely, efficiently and with due consideration to the local environment. Operations shall be conducted in such a way that the charter conditions are complied with.

Responsibility for cargo and ballast operations is included in the documented job description of a designated officer / PIC. Adequate manning levels ensure effective implementation of the cargo / ballast plan.

There is documented procedure for planning cargo and ballast operations and the master approves each operation. Where applicable this includes procedures for heavy weather ballast, and the process for the Master’s approval of each cargo and ballast operation.

Pre-arrival planning incorporates stability and stress checking at all stages of the proposed operation, including any limitation on the number and location of slack tanks. Stress, draft and trim are regularly monitored throughout the cargo operation. This allows comparison of actual and calculated stress, draft and trim to provide a proactive warning of any unplanned on unobserved deviations from the plan.

Additional information may include all details relating to load/discharge rates, ballast operations, ullages, trim, cargo stowage and management of tank atmosphere, static precautions and clearing shore lines in accordance with applicable legislation and Industry Codes of Practice, e.g. IGC, BHC, IBC, ISGOTT. It is available at the ship-shore interface meeting and complied with ISGOTT recommendations. Communications and coordination between ship and shore are discussed.

Procedures for ballast operations include comprehensive and detailed plans relating to the heavy-weather ballasting. Such plans include consideration of ballasting before the onset of severe weather. Other factors include, but are not limited to, deck access throughout such operations; supervision by an officer in communication with the bridge and deck personnel; line and valve settings; tank venting; and ullage monitoring.

Procedures ensure independent monitoring of tank levels in addition to the primary gauging system. Suitable methods include, where applicable, the routine monitoring of automatic and/or manual tank ullages / soundings, independent high and high-high level-alarms.

Ref: 06 Vessel operations

Mooring and winch brakes

The Company is committed to ensuring that all ships operating under its management are safely and securely moored at all times, when the vessel is alongside a berth, another ship or a structure.

This commitment is made in line with current legislation, industry practices and OCIMF guidelines and recommendations.

The vessel shall be safely moored in a manner to meet the requirements of the port and good seamanship, with due regard to the prevailing circumstances and conditions

Special attention shall be given to any limitations imposed on the vessel, by the non-availability of securing points ashore and extra moorings placed to compensate for such limitations.

To comply with this policy, the basic principles defined in the Company procedures are applicable to all sizes and types of ships engaged in world-wide trading.

On all vessels, winch brakes are to be tested for their ‘Brake Rendering Capacity’ (BRC) on an annual basis, and records of tests maintained.

Documented procedures include:

  • Ensuring mooring equipment and practices comply with statutory regulations, and/or industry best practices. Guidance ensures protection of personnel and safe operation of equipment during mooring operations.
  • Obtaining and recording all weather, tide, and current ranges and forecasts, and where applicable, traffic movement.
  • Awareness of mooring deployment and monitoring includes suitable supervision, competency of personnel, sufficient members in mooring teams and familiarity with any specific shore requirements relating to shore moorings, passing traffic or tidal concerns.
  • Anchoring operations which include reference to all industry practices and guidelines.
  • Ensuring that power supplies for mooring equipment, including steam, hydraulic or electric types are sufficient and adequately protected.

There is a documented procedure to ensure that maintenance of mooring equipment is completed annually. Ship-specific test kits and methods ensure that OCIMF guidelines are met. Special attention to the rendering values of mooring-winch brakes will reduce the risk of injury from overstressed moorings. For inland operation, the testing of fitted mooring equipment is carried out as regulated.

Planned maintenance systems (PMS)

Each vessel in the fleet is covered by a planned maintenance and defect reporting system. The system, which generally will be computer-based, covers all on-board equipment including all navigation, deck, engine, cargo handling and electronic equipment and machinery.

The list of Equipment in PMS includes those in vessels Class survey item list (as these are surveyable)

Adequate resources are provided in support of all maintenance activities. Supporting records for all maintenance activities are to be maintained as far as practicable, including those for vibration, infrared or performance monitoring of main and auxiliary equipment if carried out.

The maintenance and defect reporting system:

  • Monitors the vessel’s spare inventory and highlights any shortages. Those spares related to critical equipment (including environmentally critical) are given special attention
  • Tracks all fleet-wide outstanding maintenance and defect items
  • Alerts the staff responsible for fleet maintenance on board and ashore when it becomes due
  • Tracks all outstanding repair items, including dry-dock work lists

The PMS covers all mooring equipment and includes routine condition monitoring, description and method for each maintenance task, the spares inventory and the replenishment of used parts.

The (Fleet) Management in support of the above

  • Regularly reviews the vessel and fleet maintenance activity system.
  • Monitors Conditions of Class (COC) or equivalent and ensures that they are closed as soon as possible.
  • Monitors accuracy of all ship certificates
  • Ensure follow up on all required maintenance and visit ships to check the maintenance and defect correction plans and confirm company’s standards are maintained.

Emergency preparedness and response

General

The Company realises that support to vessels is vital during periods of shipboard contingencies and this must be coupled with global readiness to respond all times quickly.

The management system, in support of the above provides for measures ensuring that the Company’s shore-based and shipboard organisations can respond at any time to hazards, accidents and emergency situations, especially those involving their ships in an effective prompt and coordinated manner.

The Company has thus established, implemented and maintains documented procedures, both shipboard and shore-based, to identify potential emergency situations and potential accidents that can have an impact on health, safety, quality and the environment, and how it will respond to them.

These include effective notification procedures and communication links for alerting the emergency-response team. The system ensures there is 24-hour cover that takes account of holidays and work-related travel arrangements.

Individuals including contractors and visitors are familiarised and trained in their designated emergency response roles. Designated, authorised senior personnel receive media training to ensure accuracy and control of media releases, where applicable.

Ref: Emergency response manual

Business continuity plan

Contingency plans

The Company has detailed shore and vessel contingency plans that cover all credible emergency scenarios.

Vessel and shore-based contingency plans clearly identify roles, responsibilities and record-keeping procedures. These identified roles include communications and logistics personnel.

Such plans also identify alternate members for key positions who can stand in for the main members. All such personnel are adequately trained and are involved in drills/exercises described below.

Periodical reviews of the plans are carried out by responsible persons ashore in consultation with the vessels crew and lessons learnt from exercises and actual incidents are incorporated into the contingency plans when they are updated. The company records lessons learnt, subsequent improvements, corrective actions and resolution.

Significant improvements required may also be discussed in Management Review meetings.

Ref: Emergency response manual

Drills and exercises

The company carried out Drills/exercises to ensure both shipboard and shore staff including visitors and contractors are familiar with their duties and act promptly/effectively in case they are to respond to any emergency.

Such drills may be shipboard or ship-shore (called an exercise) and these are carried out in a planned manner and agreed upon through an Annual Drill plan. Unscheduled drills are also additionally sometimes carried out to gauge prompt and efficient response (where considered necessary).

The frequency of such drills and exercises are determined by the number of vessels within the fleet. Situations may arise when such frequencies may have to be increased. A major ship-shore exercise is to be carried out at least annually.

The scope of an exercise is consistent with the size of the fleet and its trading pattern. A matrix showing the annual exercise plan is available for comparison with the completed exercises.

Incident scenarios for exercises and drills are to fully test the contingency plans. Planning ensures that scenarios are credible and realistic. The scenarios for both shipboard drills and ship-shore exercises are to have varied content and duration.

A review process identifies any shortcomings and allows experience sharing. The findings are circulated as appropriate and significant findings if any are shared.

Exercises:

  • Are to provide a comprehensive test of all communication and mobilisation systems. To test this, some communication and mobilisation exercises are conducted outside normal office hours.
  • Allow the participation of a significant number of individuals. Ship staff on leave may be used to play the role of Master during an exercise.
  • Test the effectiveness of arrangements to call on external consultants and resources as far is possible. Communication links to external resources are checked regularly during the exercises.
  • Are documented and analysed to identify lessons learnt.
  • Include alternative members in the Emergency Response Team

Records are maintained of participants who have been involved in Emergency Drills and exercises.

Alternative members for key positions in the emergency response team are identified and trained. Lists of staff, who can perform these duties, with their contact details is maintained.

The company has in place necessary arrangements to use external resources in an emergency. Contact details for salvage, towing, media consultants and technical departments of classification societies, flag states and local authorities are readily available.

External or additional resources are on occasion used to provide more realistic drills and exercises.

Hazard identification, risk assessment and risk control

The Company has established and maintains documented procedures for the identification of hazards, the assessment of risks, and the implementation of necessary control measures. These procedures take into account; (But not limited to)

  • Routine and non-routine activities
  • Health and Hygiene risks where appropriate
  • Anticipated mooring arrangement and equipment to ensure Safety of personnel
  • Activities of all personnel having access to the workplace (including contractors and visitors)
  • Human behavior, capabilities, work culture, rest hours and other factors
  • Identified hazards originating outside the vessel, capable of affecting the health and safety of persons on-board as well as on-board work activities which may create hazards in the vicinity of the vessel
  • Infrastructure, equipment and other facilities at the workplace, whether provided by the company or others
  • All other operational hazards covering safety, environmental, security impacts
  • Actual or proposed changes in the management system and changes in the knowledge and information about hazards
  • Past incidents, internal or external to the organisation, including emergencies and their causes
  • Legal and other applicable requirements

The Company’s methodology for hazard identification and risk assessment:

  • Is by its scope, nature and timing proactive rather than reactive
  • Provides for monitoring of physical hazards, inventorying hazardous materials and assessing human factors
  • Requires records of all valid/current assessments to be maintained on-board
  • Provides for the classification of risks and identification of those that are to be eliminated or controlled by measures as defined in OHS objectives and management programs
  • Ensures all Risks mitigation measures are identified through Risk Assessment based on the hierarchy of controls and are implemented prior to commencement of work
  • Provides input into the determination of facility requirements, identification of training needs and/or development of operational controls, and improving the operating procedures in the Management System
  • Includes provision for assessing new or non-routine tasks.

The Company ensures that the results of these assessments and the effects of these controls ashore are considered when setting its OHS objectives. Records of all risk assessments and meetings are reviewed ashore and kept on file Where required these are modified. Relevant documents are reissued in part, or as a whole, if required, after each review

The Risk-assessment processes include response elements to limit the impact of any unplanned occurrences. Contingency plans have been developed to facilitate Safe Management and recovery of such situations as described in company’s Contingency Manual

The company identifies best practices for common areas of risk assessment and ensures that these are shared across the fleet.

The company maintains a database of all ship-generated and shore-generated risk assessments.

The Company has prescribed levels for the use of PPE appropriate to the work being undertaken and the cargoes being carried by their vessels in support of Risk control measures

The system includes permits for the control of any hot work onboard the vessel, including requirements for office-management review of hot work in identified hazardous areas. Other permits, such as enclosed space entry, are also used – refer to ISGOTT (For Tankers)

Carriage of dangerous goods in bulk or packaged form

In recognising the potential dangers associated with the carriage of Dangerous Cargoes, either in bulk or packaged form, the Company requires that the Master and Officers exercise due diligence in the preparation for, and the carriage and discharge of such goods.

  • SOLAS and MARPOL conventions
  • International Maritime Dangerous Goods code (IMDG)
  • IMO Code of practice for solid bulk cargoes

Incinerators, and other waste/garbage disposal and/or treatment equipment, must be maintained in full operational condition, and operated in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions, and in compliance with applicable National and International regulations.

Care is taken to ensure specific local/regional requirements are additionally complied with.

Management of change

The Company has established a process for the implementation and control of planned changes that impact QHSE performance. Situations where the “Management of Change” process would be considered applicable are defined.

The management of change process:

  • Ensures that potential consequences of a change are identified, together with any required Risk-deduction measures
  • Clearly defines the level of authority required for the approval of a change.
  • Ensures changes are reviewed at the defined level of authority and, where new risks are identified, the adequacy of the risk-reduction measures, is authorised by a higher authority. The appropriate level of approval required for different categories of change is based on the level of risk involved.
  • Ensures that changes if not carried out within the proposed time scale are reviewed and revalidated, and that temporary changes do not exceed the initial authorisation for scope or time without review and re-approval by the appropriate level of management.
  • Ensures that the documentation supporting a change includes the reason for the change, a clear understanding of the safety and environmental implications, and the appropriate level of approval. Any change is approved by senior ships' management/responsible person and not by the person directly involved in the change. The document includes reference to appropriate safety and environmental issues.
  • Identifies all personnel that may be affected by the change and ensures that those identified understand the extent and likely impact of any planned change.
  • Ensures that training needs arising from changes to equipment or procedures are identified and documented. All appropriate personnel receive the required training within a specific period.
  • Links with, and ties into the document-control system, so that important controlled documentation remains up to date.

When organisational changes (changes in reporting relationships, elimination of positions, restructuring, etc.), take place, the personnel responsible for supervising or managing the function(s) undergoing change are made responsible for clear and explicit reassignment of responsibilities to appropriate personnel.

There are documented staff handover procedures for both shore-based personnel and ships' crews. The scope and depth of the handover process is relevant to the responsibilities of the personnel involved.
